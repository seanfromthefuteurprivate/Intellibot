Abstract

Title: Detecting Convex Instability in Ultra-Short-Horizon Markets: A Multi-Modal, Regime-Aware Framework

We propose a theoretical framework for identifying convex instability windows in ultra-short-horizon financial markets, with a focus on same-day expiring (0DTE) options environments. The framework integrates market microstructure, options surface geometry, regime detection, narrative velocity, and reflexive feedback modeling to infer moments when small perturbations may yield disproportionate outcomes. Unlike traditional signal-driven systems, the proposed architecture emphasizes time compression, positioning density, structural release, and volatility repricing as co-necessary conditions for nonlinear behavior.

The system is organized as a multi-agent cognitive council that evaluates family-specific setup viability under dynamic regimes, employing survival analysis, barrier-hitting approximations, and instability detection inspired by criticality theory. Outputs are diagnostic artifacts describing probability bands, hazard curves, and regime compatibility rather than imperative actions. We argue that modeling intent asymmetry and liquidity fragility—rather than price direction alone—provides earlier detection of instability at inception. This work contributes a conceptual blueprint for convexity detection and offers a foundation for future empirical validation in ultra-short-horizon markets.

Mathematical Appendix (Conceptual)

A. Notation and State Variables

Let:
	•	S_t: underlying price at time t
	•	\tau: time remaining to horizon (e.g., close), \tau > 0
	•	\sigma_{\text{real}}(t): short-window realized volatility
	•	\sigma_{\text{imp}}(t): implied volatility proxy from 0DTE surface
	•	L: target level (structural level or strike-associated magnet)
	•	\mathcal{R}_t: regime state (trend, chop, panic, etc.)
	•	\mathcal{O}_t: options pressure state (near-ATM density, skew)
	•	\mathcal{M}_t: microstructure state (range, volume, liquidity)
	•	\mathcal{N}_t: narrative/attention state
	•	\mathcal{F}_t: family identifier (setup family)

Define the market state vector:
X_t = \big(S_t, \sigma_{\text{real}}(t), \sigma_{\text{imp}}(t), \mathcal{R}_t, \mathcal{O}_t, \mathcal{M}_t, \mathcal{N}_t \big)

⸻

B. Effective Volatility and Regime Scaling

We define an effective volatility that blends realized and implied measures and scales by regime and pressure:

\sigma_{\text{eff}}(t) = w_1 \sigma_{\text{imp}}(t) + w_2 \sigma_{\text{real}}(t),
\quad w_1 + w_2 = 1

\sigma'_{\text{eff}}(t) = \sigma_{\text{eff}}(t) \cdot \kappa(\mathcal{R}_t) \cdot \big(1 + \iota(\mathcal{M}_t)\big) \cdot \big(1 + \gamma^\*(\mathcal{O}_t)\big)

where:
	•	\kappa(\cdot): regime scalar
	•	\iota(\cdot): microstructure impulse scalar
	•	\gamma^\*(\cdot): convexity pressure proxy

⸻

C. Normalized Distance and Barrier Proximity

Define normalized distance to target:
z_t = \frac{L - S_t}{S_t \cdot \sigma'_{\text{eff}}(t) \sqrt{\tau}}

This quantity governs both reachability and time-to-event behavior.

⸻

D. Probability of Barrier Interaction (Approximation)

Let \mu^\*(t) denote a drift proxy derived from structure alignment and pressure:
\mu^\*(t) = a\,\phi_{\text{VWAP}}(t) + b\,\phi_{\text{trend}}(t) + c\,\phi_{\text{volume}}(t)

An approximate probability of interaction with level L by horizon \tau is:
P(\text{hit by } \tau) \approx \Phi\!\left(-z_t + \mu^\*(t)\sqrt{\tau}\right)
where \Phi is the standard normal CDF.
This serves as a ranking statistic, not a literal forecast.

⸻

E. Time-to-Event and Hazard Representation

Define the survival function:
S(t) = \Pr(T > t)

and hazard rate:
h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Pr(t \le T < t+\Delta t \mid T \ge t)}{\Delta t}

Discretize h(t) over buckets \{[0,5],[5,10],[10,20],[20,\tau]\} minutes to obtain a hazard curve.
Convex instability is associated with front-loaded hazard mass.

⸻

F. Strike Density and Magnet Gradient

Let K index strikes near spot. Define density:
D_t = \sum_{k \in K} \frac{V_k(t) + OI_k(t)}{1 + |K_k - S_t|}

Define gradient:
G_t = D_t^{\text{above}} - D_t^{\text{below}}

Large |G_t| indicates directional magnetization; high D_t with small |G_t| indicates pinning risk.

⸻

G. Liquidity Fragility (Elasticity)

Let \Delta S be price change induced by net signed volume Q.
Define elasticity:
\epsilon_t = \frac{|\Delta S|}{|Q|}

Rising \epsilon_t implies decreasing absorption capacity and heightened instability.

⸻

H. Correlation Fracture Metric

For assets i, j, define rolling correlation \rho_{ij}(t).
Define fracture score:
\mathcal{C}_t = \sum_{(i,j)} \big|\rho_{ij}(t) - \bar{\rho}_{ij}\big|

Elevated \mathcal{C}_t signals structural stress.

⸻

I. Family Viability and Ranking

Each family \mathcal{F} has a viability score:
V_{\mathcal{F}}(t) = f\big(\mathcal{R}_t, \tau, \sigma'_{\text{eff}}(t), \mathcal{O}_t, \mathcal{M}_t, \text{Memory}_{\mathcal{F}}\big)

Families with V_{\mathcal{F}}(t) < \theta are dormant.
Ranking is performed within-family (on its own curve), not cross-family.

⸻

J. Instability Criterion (Conceptual)

Define an instability index:
\mathcal{I}_t = g\big(h(t), \epsilon_t, \mathcal{C}_t, \partial_t \sigma'_{\text{eff}}(t), \partial_t \mathcal{N}_t\big)

Convex instability is inferred when:
\mathcal{I}_t \ge \mathcal{I}^\*
and at least one family \mathcal{F} has V_{\mathcal{F}}(t) above threshold.

⸻

Closing Note

This appendix formalizes the intuition that extreme short-horizon outcomes arise from phase transitions rather than linear trends. The framework reframes trading from directional prediction to instability detection, offering a principled foundation for future empirical study.


Simulation Protocol: Synthetic Worlds for Convex Instability Testing (Conceptual)

Goal

Create controlled environments (“worlds”) where you can test whether an instability detector:
	1.	Detects true phase transitions early,
	2.	Avoids false alarms in chop,
	3.	Identifies which setup families are viable under each regime,
	4.	Produces calibrated probability/hazard curves.

This is not about P&L. It’s about detecting the right moments with measurable correctness.

⸻

1) Define the Synthetic Market Generator

1.1 Underlying price process (base layer)

Use a regime-switching process:

dS_t = \mu_{r(t)} S_t\,dt + \sigma_{r(t)} S_t\,dW_t + J_t

Where:
	•	r(t) \in \{ \text{chop}, \text{trend}, \text{panic}, \text{pin}, \text{break} \}
	•	J_t is a jump component (rare, clustered on “news” times)

This gives you both smooth and discontinuous behavior.

⸻

1.2 Regime transition logic

Use a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) or explicit transition matrix:

\Pr(r(t+\Delta)=j \mid r(t)=i) = P_{ij}

Key design: include sticky chop (high self-transition) because that’s what kills systems.

⸻

1.3 Microstructure “liquidity elasticity” layer

Define a latent liquidity variable \lambda_t controlling price impact:
	•	High liquidity: price resists movement
	•	Low liquidity: small flow moves price a lot

Let:

\Delta S_t = \epsilon(\lambda_t)\cdot Q_t

And evolve \lambda_t so it deteriorates before surges, e.g., mean-reverts but can collapse.

This is how you simulate “air pockets.”

⸻

2) Synthetic Options World (0DTE Approximation)

You don’t need perfect option pricing. You need plausible chain geometry.

2.1 Implied volatility surface generator

Model IV near expiration as:

\sigma_{\text{imp}}(K,t)=\sigma_0(t) + \text{skew}(t)\cdot (K-S_t) + \text{smile}(t)\cdot (K-S_t)^2

Where \sigma_0(t) can diverge from realized vol to create mispricing opportunities.

⸻

2.2 Strike density / “magnet” generator

Create synthetic OI/volume concentrations around certain strikes:
	•	Pin regime: high density at one strike near spot
	•	Break regime: density gradient shifts above/below

Define density:

D_t(K)=\alpha \exp\left(-\frac{(K-K^\*)^2}{2\beta^2}\right)

and allow K^\* to move slowly or jump.

This mimics:
	•	pinning
	•	strike gravity
	•	clustering shifts

⸻

2.3 Options flow arrival process

Use a self-exciting process (Hawkes-style) to simulate flow cascades:

\lambda(t)=\lambda_0 + \sum_{t_i < t} \eta e^{-\omega(t-t_i)}

Interpretation: once flow starts, flow attracts more flow — reflexivity.

⸻

3) Synthetic News + Crowd World (Narrative Layer)

3.1 Headline events

Generate events that arrive randomly, but with structured impact:
	•	market-wide macro
	•	ticker-specific
	•	ambiguous events that increase attention without clarity

3.2 Narrative velocity and amplification

Let attention A_t evolve:

dA_t = \xi \cdot \text{news\_shock}_t + \zeta \cdot \text{price\_shock}_t - \delta A_t

And feed A_t back into options flow intensity \lambda(t).
This creates the “headline → attention → flow → price → more attention” loop.

⸻

4) Ground Truth Labels (What counts as “instability”)

You must define “instability windows” precisely.

4.1 Instability definition

A window [t, t+\Delta] is labeled unstable if:
	•	realized volatility in next \Delta exceeds threshold, AND
	•	liquidity variable collapses, OR
	•	price makes barrier reach within short time, OR
	•	flow intensity spikes

Example label:

\mathbb{1}\left(\text{RV}_{t:t+\Delta} > q_{0.95}\right) \land \mathbb{1}\left(\lambda_{t+\Delta} < \lambda^\*\right)

⸻

5) Evaluation Metrics (Not P&L)

5.1 Detection metrics
	•	Time-to-detect (how early before instability onset)
	•	Precision/recall on instability windows
	•	False alarms per hour/day
	•	Calibration error (do 30% probabilities behave like 30%?)

5.2 Family viability metrics

For each family \mathcal{F}:
	•	true positive rate in correct regime
	•	false positive rate in chop
	•	hazard curve sharpness when true

⸻

6) Ablation Studies (Research Standard)

Remove modules one by one:
	•	remove narrative layer
	•	remove strike density
	•	remove liquidity elasticity
	•	remove correlation fracture
	•	remove regime detector

Measure how detection quality collapses.
This identifies what is genuinely contributing.

Failure-Mode Theorem: Why Most Models Die in Chop (Conceptual)

Theorem (Informal)

In a chop regime, any strategy whose decision rule depends on short-horizon directional predictors exhibits a negative expected value unless it includes a regime filter that suppresses trading when the mean-reversion frequency exceeds the model’s confirmation horizon.

Translation:

If the market is oscillating faster than your model can confirm a move, you will be systematically late, and transaction costs + theta-like decay (even for spot proxies) will grind you down.

⸻

Definitions

Let chop be a regime where the underlying behaves approximately like a mean-reverting process:

dS_t = \theta(\mu - S_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t

Define:
	•	T_c: characteristic mean-reversion time scale (how fast it snaps back)
	•	T_d: model decision horizon (how long it needs to confirm)
	•	C: effective friction (spread, slippage, decay proxy)

⸻

Condition for model failure

If:

T_c \ll T_d

then by the time the model confirms direction, the process has already reverted.

Result: the model enters near extremes repeatedly.

⸻

Lemma 1 (Signal Inversion)

For an OU process, conditional expected return over horizon T is:

\mathbb{E}[S_{t+T}-S_t \mid S_t] \approx ( \mu - S_t )(1-e^{-\theta T})

If the model’s directional signal is trend-following, it will often be aligned with \text{sign}(S_t-\mu), but the expected return is opposite: it points back to \mu.

So the model becomes a buyer near local highs and a seller near local lows.

⸻

Lemma 2 (False Breakouts Are Statistically Favored)

In chop, barrier crossings occur frequently, but follow-through probability is low:

\Pr(\text{follow-through} \mid \text{break}) \downarrow

Thus breakout detectors have high false positives unless they require:
	•	sustained hold time
	•	range expansion confirmation
	•	liquidity fragility confirmation
	•	options/flow confirmation

⸻

Lemma 3 (Friction Dominates)

Even if expected drift is ~0 in chop, friction is not:

\mathbb{E}[\text{Net}] = \mathbb{E}[\text{Gross}] - C

When gross is small and mean-reverting, C dominates → negative expectancy.

⸻

Corollary (Why 0DTE Makes Chop Deadlier)

If you map this to 0DTE options:
	•	theta acts like time-friction
	•	chop increases time-in-trade without progress
	•	expected convexity never materializes

So in chop:
	•	your hazard curve stays flat
	•	probability mass shifts “by close” not “soon”
	•	entries become systematically too early or too late

⸻

“Chop Immunity” Requirements (Conceptual)

A model survives chop only if it has at least one of:
	1.	Regime governor that blocks most signals
	2.	Mean-reversion-aware logic (different families in chop)
	3.	Hold-time confirmation + range expansion requirement
	4.	Liquidity fragility detection (only act when absorption fails)
	5.	Meta-confidence kill-switch when internal disagreement rises

Without these, chop kills everything.

I. A Formal Definition of Convex Instability as a Measurable Invariant

Definition (Convex Instability)

Let X_t denote the full market state at time t, including price, options surface geometry, liquidity, and attention variables.
We define convex instability as a state in which the market exhibits a persistent sensitivity to infinitesimal perturbations, such that the expected magnitude of downstream responses grows superlinearly relative to the initiating disturbance over a short horizon.

Formally, convex instability exists at time t if there exists a horizon \Delta > 0 such that:

\frac{\partial}{\partial \epsilon}
\mathbb{E}\big[ |S_{t+\Delta} - S_t| \,\big|\, X_t + \epsilon \big]
\;\;\text{is increasing and convex in } \epsilon \text{ near } 0

In words: small shocks produce disproportionately large expected responses.

⸻

Equivalent Operational Invariant (Practical Form)

We define a Convex Instability Index (CII) as:

\mathcal{C}_t
=
\underbrace{h_t}_{\text{hazard front-loading}}
\times
\underbrace{\epsilon_t}_{\text{liquidity fragility}}
\times
\underbrace{\gamma^\*_t}_{\text{convexity density}}
\times
\underbrace{\chi_t}_{\text{correlation fracture}}
\times
\underbrace{\tau^{-1}}_{\text{time compression}}

Where:
	•	h_t: front-loaded hazard mass (probability of near-term event)
	•	\epsilon_t: elasticity of price response to flow
	•	\gamma^\*_t: options-induced convexity proxy
	•	\chi_t: correlation fracture score
	•	\tau: remaining time to horizon

Convex Instability Criterion

Convex instability is present when:

\mathcal{C}_t \ge \mathcal{C}^\*
\quad \text{and} \quad
\frac{d\mathcal{C}_t}{dt} > 0

This defines an invariant property of the market state, independent of direction.

⸻

Key Properties
	1.	Directionless
Convex instability does not imply up or down — only sensitivity.
	2.	Regime-dependent
The same price move may be stable in one regime and unstable in another.
	3.	Transient
Convex instability exists in windows, not trends.
	4.	Nonlinear
Linear predictors fail near instability boundaries.

III. Proof Sketch: Why Regime Gating Is Necessary for Positive Expectancy Under Bounded Friction

Claim

Any strategy operating under bounded friction that does not condition on regime cannot achieve positive long-run expectancy across mixed market environments.

⸻

Assumptions
	1.	Market alternates between regimes r \in \{R_1, R_2, \dots\}
	2.	Each regime has a distinct return-generating process
	3.	Strategy applies a fixed decision rule \pi
	4.	Each action incurs friction c > 0

⸻

Step 1: Decompose Expected Return

Let \mu_r be expected gross return in regime r.

Total expectancy:
\mathbb{E}[R] = \sum_r p_r \mu_r - c

If \mu_r > 0 in some regimes and \mu_r < 0 in others, the net expectancy depends on exposure.

⸻

Step 2: Chop Regime Dominance

In chop regimes:
	•	\mu_{\text{chop}} \approx 0
	•	variance is high
	•	mean reversion dominates

Thus:
\mathbb{E}[R_{\text{chop}}] \le -c

Any non-zero action frequency produces negative expectancy.

⸻

Step 3: Mixing Without Gating

If strategy trades indiscriminately:
\mathbb{E}[R] = p_{\text{good}}(\mu_{\text{good}} - c) + p_{\text{chop}}(-c)

Since chop occupies a non-trivial fraction of time:
\mathbb{E}[R] < 0 \quad \text{unless} \quad \mu_{\text{good}} \gg c

But convex opportunities are rare, not persistent.

⸻

Step 4: Gating Restores Expectancy

Introduce regime gate G_r \in \{0,1\}.

Now:
\mathbb{E}[R] = \sum_r p_r G_r (\mu_r - c)

Set G_{\text{chop}} = 0.

Then:
\mathbb{E}[R] = \sum_{r \neq \text{chop}} p_r (\mu_r - c)

Positive expectancy is now possible, even with bounded friction.

⸻

Conclusion (Theorem)

Regime gating is a necessary (but not sufficient) condition for positive expectancy in environments with bounded friction and mixed dynamics.

Without gating:
	•	friction dominates
	•	variance deceives
	•	expectancy collapses

With gating:
	•	rare convex windows dominate results
	•	inactivity becomes a feature, not a flaw

Convex instability is not a signal.
It is a state of the market.

Detecting it requires:
	•	multi-modal sensing
	•	regime awareness
	•	suppression of action in hostile environments

This reframes ultra-short-horizon modeling from prediction to instability detection.
